# Exhale Academy CSE Hard Authoring Checklist

Use this checklist when reviewing or writing any CSE case. The goal is to make the case feel like the actual NBRC CSE: neutral, staged, operational, and sequence-driven.

## 1. Stem and Prompt Tone
- Does the stem sound clinically neutral rather than educational or editorial?
- Does the prompt avoid authored phrases like:
  - `best available`
  - `structured plan`
  - `causes-first response`
  - `escalation readiness`
- Does the prompt use NBRC-like phrasing such as:
  - `Which of the following should be evaluated?`
  - `Which of the following should be recommended?`
  - `Which of the following should be done FIRST?`

## 2. Data Release
- Does the case reveal only enough information for the current decision?
- Is the case staged as:
  - presentation
  - action
  - response
  - next decision
- Is there any data that should be held back until after the first intervention?

## 3. Decision Type Separation
- Is each section clearly one of:
  - assessment/evaluation
  - recommendation/intervention
  - reassessment
  - disposition/follow-up
- Does the case avoid mixing assessment and treatment in a single question unless clinically necessary?

## 4. Single-Choice Option Design
- Does each single-choice option represent one decision?
- Are any options over-bundled with multiple actions, settings, or rationales?
- If ventilator settings are requested, is it clearly a settings question rather than a general management question?

## 5. Select-Many Option Design
- Are the truly indicated selections limited and realistic?
- Is the max-select count small and exam-like?
  - preferred: 2-4
  - caution: 5+
  - avoid: defaulting to 8
- Do options include plausible but non-indicated distractors?

## 6. Sequencing and “FIRST”
- If the question says `FIRST`, is the correct answer the true immediate stabilizing move?
- Are there any options that are good actions but out of sequence?
- Does the case punish delay appropriately?

## 7. Distractor Quality
- Are wrong options plausible in another context?
- Are distractors wrong because they are:
  - premature
  - delayed
  - incomplete
  - too risky
  - low-yield
  - not indicated now
- Avoid distractors that are cartoonishly bad unless the real exam would plausibly include them.

## 8. Objective Trigger Logic
- Are escalation decisions tied to objective findings like:
  - worsening pH
  - rising PaCO2
  - falling oxygenation
  - worsening work of breathing
  - declining mental status
  - apnea/bradycardia
- Is the escalation justified by current physiology rather than author preference?

## 9. Reassessment
- After an intervention, does the case show updated vitals, ABG, SpO2, mental status, breath sounds, or device response?
- Does the next question use those updated findings?
- Does the case avoid “one and done” treatment logic without follow-up?

## 10. Testing Yield
- Is each test or assessment high-yield for the current problem?
- Are there any tests included because they are medically interesting but not decision-relevant?
- Do wrong answers include low-yield or unnecessary tests in a realistic way?

## 11. Explanation Style
- Is the explanation short and scoring-oriented?
- Does it explain why the answer is indicated or not indicated now?
- Does it avoid drifting into textbook teaching?

## 12. Population Fit
- Does the vocabulary fit the patient population?
  - adult ICU
  - outpatient asthma/COPD
  - neonatal
  - pediatric
  - neuro/neuromuscular
- Does the case preserve a consistent exam voice while using the right clinical terms for that population?

## 13. Disposition and Follow-Up
- If the case reaches disposition, are follow-up recommendations concrete and practical?
- Does outpatient/discharge planning stay operational rather than vague?

## 14. Final Classification
After review, classify the case:
- `Rewrite in place`
  - clinical skeleton is good
  - wording/options need realism adjustment
- `Heavy refactor`
  - case concept is good
  - sequencing, option bundling, and selection structure need major rework
- `Rebuild`
  - decision model is fundamentally wrong
  - faster to recreate than salvage
