Exhale Academy CSE Review
Case 12 -- COPD Non-Critical (Chronic Bronchitis-Predominant Flare)

Intro
Noncritical COPD exacerbation with productive cough, secretion burden, and possible infective trigger requiring acute treatment and safe discharge planning.

Stem
Chronic-bronchitis-predominant COPD flare with productive cough and moderate hypoxemia.

Step 1
A 61-year-old man with severe COPD comes to the emergency department because of worsening dyspnea and increased yellow-green sputum.

While breathing room air, the following are noted:
HR 106/min
RR 28/min
BP 152/94 mm Hg
Temperature 38.1 C
SpO2 86%

He has a frequent productive cough.

Which of the following should be evaluated initially? SELECT AS MANY AS INDICATED (MAX 3).

A. Sputum amount and character (+2)
B. Breath sounds and work of breathing (+2)
C. Pulse oximetry, temperature, and vital signs (+2)
D. Exercise oximetry (-3)
E. Routine CT scan before treatment (-3)

High-score progression: Productive cough persists, and moderate hypoxemia remains present.
Default progression: Assessment is incomplete. Dyspnea and secretion burden persist.

Step 2
Breath sounds reveal diffuse rhonchi and scattered wheezes. Which of the following should be recommended FIRST?

A. Administer controlled oxygen and short-acting bronchodilator therapy (+3)
B. Start antibiotics as the only initial treatment (-2)
C. Observe without treatment because the patient is still alert (-3)
D. Proceed directly to intubation (-3)

High-score progression: Breathing improves slightly, but productive cough remains.
Default progression: Symptoms remain unstable, and oxygenation improves little.

Step 3
Thirty minutes after controlled oxygen and bronchodilator therapy are started, the patient remains productive but less dyspneic.

While receiving O2 by nasal cannula at 2 L/min, the following are noted:
HR 102/min
RR 26/min
Temperature 37.9 C
SpO2 89%

ABG analysis reveals:
pH 7.35
PaCO2 54 torr
PaO2 60 torr
HCO3- 29 mEq/L

Which of the following should be evaluated now? SELECT AS MANY AS INDICATED (MAX 3).

A. Sputum burden and effectiveness of cough (+2)
B. Breath sounds and work of breathing (+2)
C. ABG and oxygen saturation trend (+2)
D. Pulmonary rehabilitation schedule (-3)
E. Discharge paperwork (-3)

High-score progression: Productive cough persists, but the patient remains stable.
Default progression: Reassessment is delayed, and symptoms persist.

Step 4
Productive cough and dyspnea persist, but the patient remains alert. Which of the following should be recommended now?

A. Add systemic corticosteroid therapy and continue bronchodilator treatment (+3)
B. Stop bronchodilator therapy because wheezing decreased (-3)
C. Proceed directly to intubation (-3)
D. Delay additional therapy until tomorrow (-3)

High-score progression: Breathing becomes easier, and cough burden begins to improve.
Default progression: Symptoms remain persistent, and discharge readiness is uncertain.

Step 5
After additional treatment, the patient is breathing more comfortably. Which of the following should be evaluated before disposition? SELECT AS MANY AS INDICATED (MAX 3).

A. Stable oxygen requirement and sustained symptom improvement (+2)
B. Inhaler technique and medication adherence (+2)
C. Return precautions and follow-up plan (+2)
D. Routine antibiotics without infection assessment (-3)
E. Discharge without addressing home oxygen needs (-3)

High-score progression: Symptoms remain improved, and discharge planning is complete.
Default progression: Discharge readiness remains incomplete.

Step 6
Symptoms are improving and oxygenation is stable. Which of the following should be recommended postdischarge?

A. Discharge with follow-up and COPD action planning after sustained stability (+3)
B. Admit for monitored care if dyspnea or oxygen needs worsen again (+1)
C. Discharge without follow-up (-3)
D. Place in hallway observation without a treatment plan (-3)

High-score progression: Final outcome: the patient is discharged with follow-up and COPD action planning.
Default progression: Final outcome: the discharge plan is inadequate, and symptoms recur early.
