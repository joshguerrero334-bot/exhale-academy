# Exhale CSE Pathology Workflow Guardrails

This document exists to prevent the CSE bank from drifting into a single repeated scenario shape.

The rewrite standard for Exhale CSE now has two layers:

1. `Case 6 realism standard`
- neutral NBRC-style phrasing
- smaller, more realistic answer sets
- cleaner vitals and ABG presentation
- objective outcome wording
- tighter reassessment cadence

2. `Pathology workflow standard`
- the disease process determines the case arc
- the escalation logic must match the pathology
- the ending must feel earned by the physiology and clinical context

The realism standard controls how the case sounds.
The pathology workflow standard controls how the case moves.

## Core Rule
Do not make every case follow the same ending pattern.

Case progression should come from:
- the disease mechanism
- the tempo of deterioration
- the reversibility of the problem
- the expected respiratory intervention pathway
- the likely care setting after stabilization

The bank should not feel like:
- assess
- support
- reassess
- intubate
- ICU

unless the pathology truly warrants that sequence.

## Universal Authoring Principle
Every case should answer these questions before it is written or rewritten:

1. What is the main physiologic problem?
2. What is the first high-yield assessment for that problem?
3. What is the safest first intervention?
4. What change in findings should occur after that intervention?
5. What determines escalation vs continuation vs discharge?
6. What is the most pathology-appropriate final disposition?

## Pathology Family Guardrails

## 1. Obstructive Airway Disease
Includes:
- asthma
- COPD exacerbation
- bronchospasm
- chronic bronchitis flare
- emphysema flare

### Typical physiologic problem
- airflow obstruction
- dynamic hyperinflation
- work of breathing
- CO2 retention in some cases
- secretion burden in selected COPD patterns

### Common early priorities
- work of breathing
- air movement / breath sounds
- speech tolerance
- pulse oximetry
- controlled oxygen when indicated
- bronchodilator response

### Typical progression shapes
#### Noncritical asthma/COPD
- acute symptoms
- oxygen + bronchodilator therapy
- reassessment
- steroids / continued therapy
- disposition decision
- discharge if sustained improvement

#### Critical asthma/COPD
- severe distress or rising CO2
- oxygen + bronchodilator therapy
- reassessment with worsening fatigue / air movement / ABG
- escalation to NIV or intubation depending on pathology
- post-intubation management if needed
- ICU

### Distinctive rules
- not every obstructive case should intubate
- COPD and asthma should not feel identical
- emphysema-predominant cases should emphasize gas exchange and dyspnea
- chronic bronchitis cases should emphasize cough/secretion burden more heavily
- COPD oxygen strategies should be more controlled than asthma strategies

### Common endings
- discharge with action plan and follow-up
- monitored admission
- ICU after NIV failure or intubation

## 2. Pulmonary Embolism / Hemodynamic Strain
Includes:
- sudden hypoxemia with chest pain
- PE-like shock physiology
- acute right-heart strain patterns

### Typical physiologic problem
- severe V/Q mismatch
- hypoxemia
- perfusion failure
- hemodynamic instability

### Common early priorities
- oxygenation
- blood pressure / perfusion assessment
- signs of right-heart strain
- mental status
- rapid stabilization

### Typical progression shape
- sudden onset
- oxygen support
- reassessment with persistent hypoxemia and hypotension
- escalation based on shock pattern
- ongoing high-acuity monitoring
- ICU/high-acuity disposition

### Distinctive rules
- this family should feel faster and more hemodynamic than obstructive disease
- the case should not drift into a generic ventilator case unless the patient truly decompensates that far
- decision pressure should come from falling blood pressure and worsening perfusion, not just worsening SpO2

### Common endings
- ICU with definitive therapy coordination
- rarely a low-acuity destination

## 3. Upper-Airway Compromise
Includes:
- post-extubation stridor
- upper-airway edema
- severe obstruction patterns
- epiglottitis/croup-like logic depending on age/pathology if used

### Typical physiologic problem
- airway narrowing or obstruction
- rapid fatigue risk
- oxygenation decline secondary to airway compromise

### Common early priorities
- upper-airway sounds
- speech tolerance
- work of breathing
- oxygenation
- airway equipment readiness

### Typical progression shape
- noisy breathing / stridor
- rescue therapy
- brief improvement or no improvement
- worsening airway reserve
- reintubation / definitive airway if deterioration continues
- post-airway monitoring

### Distinctive rules
- this family should feel airway-first, not ABG-first
- upper-airway findings should drive the case more than lower-airway breath sounds
- the key turning point is loss of airway reserve, not prolonged medical management

### Common endings
- ICU after airway rescue
- monitored post-airway admission

## 4. Neuromuscular Ventilatory Failure
Includes:
- GBS-like weakness
- myasthenic/neuromuscular pump failure patterns
- poor cough with rising CO2 risk

### Typical physiologic problem
- ventilatory pump weakness
- low tidal volume
- retained secretions
- loss of airway protection over time
- rising CO2 and fatigue

### Common early priorities
- cough strength
- VC / inspiratory force / ventilatory mechanics
- breathing pattern
- mental status
- secretion handling
- ABG trend

### Typical progression shape
- weakness with shallow breathing
- objective mechanics assessment
- ventilatory support if appropriate
- reassessment with rising CO2 or reduced effort
- intubation when support fails or airway protection declines
- secretion management and ICU care

### Distinctive rules
- these cases should feel more mechanics-driven than classic asthma/COPD
- secretion clearance matters more than in many other families
- the escalation trigger is usually worsening pump failure, not bronchospasm
- options should reflect VC/MIP/cough effectiveness rather than generic distress language only

### Common endings
- ICU/high-acuity monitoring
- prolonged ventilatory support pathway

## 5. Trauma
Includes:
- chest trauma
- flail chest
- hemothorax/pneumothorax
- airway trauma

### Typical physiologic problem
- structural injury
- oxygenation impairment
- pain limiting ventilation
- blood loss or shock in selected cases
- tension physiology in selected cases

### Common early priorities
- airway patency
- chest rise and breath sounds
- hemodynamics
- oxygenation
- injury mechanism clues

### Typical progression shape
- trauma presentation
- immediate stabilization
- focused assessment / imaging when stable enough
- intervention based on injury pattern
- reassessment after intervention
- monitored admission / ICU / OR-type pathway depending on case

### Distinctive rules
- trauma cases should feel procedural and time-sensitive
- they should not sound like medical floor deterioration cases
- mechanism matters more here than in many adult medical scenarios

### Common endings
- ICU
- monitored trauma admission
- escalation to procedural/surgical pathway

## 6. ARDS / Severe Diffuse Parenchymal Failure
Includes:
- ARDS
- severe diffuse hypoxemic respiratory failure
- escalating ventilator oxygenation problems

### Typical physiologic problem
- refractory hypoxemia
- diffuse lung injury
- poor compliance
- oxygenation failure despite support

### Common early priorities
- oxygenation support
- ABG trend
- imaging correlation
- ventilator response
- compliance and pressure clues when intubated

### Typical progression shape
- severe hypoxemia
- escalating oxygen/ventilator support
- reassessment with persistent hypoxemia
- PEEP/FiO2 strategy and lung-protective adjustments
- ICU-level ongoing management

### Distinctive rules
- these cases should feel oxygenation/compliance-driven
- not secretion-driven
- not upper-airway-driven
- not primarily shock-driven unless mixed pathology is intentional

### Common endings
- ICU ventilator management
- continued high-acuity oxygenation strategy

## 7. Cardiovascular / Pulmonary Edema / CHF Patterns
Includes:
- pulmonary edema
- CHF decompensation
- acute cardiac respiratory distress

### Typical physiologic problem
- hydrostatic pulmonary edema
- oxygenation failure
- increased work of breathing
- possible arrhythmia/hemodynamic contribution

### Common early priorities
- oxygenation
- breath sounds
- perfusion and rhythm clues
- frothy sputum / edema findings

### Typical progression shape
- dyspnea and pulmonary edema findings
- oxygen/ventilatory support
- targeted medical treatment
- reassessment of oxygenation and work of breathing
- escalation if worsening
- monitored or ICU disposition depending on severity

### Distinctive rules
- should not feel like pure obstructive disease
- diuresis/afterload logic may matter more than bronchodilator logic
- bedside clues should point to fluid overload/cardiac cause

### Common endings
- monitored admission
- ICU if unstable or intubated

## 8. Pediatric
### Typical physiologic problem
Depends on diagnosis, but should reflect pediatric tempo and terminology.

### Distinctive rules
- pediatric cues should differ clearly from adult wording
- vital sign expectations should reflect age
- escalation thresholds and devices should fit pediatrics
- caregiver context may be appropriate if realistic

## 9. Neonatal
### Typical physiologic problem
Depends on diagnosis, but should reflect neonatal respiratory workflow.

### Distinctive rules
- use neonatal device/context language
- emphasize apnea, bradycardia, retractions, oxygenation, airway/patency, and ventilatory support appropriate to neonates
- cases should not sound like adult RT cases with smaller numbers

## Ending Diversity Guardrail
Before finalizing a case, verify that the ending is appropriate for the pathology.

### Acceptable final outcomes across the bank include:
- discharge with follow-up
- monitored admission
- ICU admission
- intubation and ICU
- post-procedural monitoring
- ongoing ventilator management
- rescue airway stabilization

### Red flags
Rewrite if the case feels like:
- every scenario ends in ICU by default
- every scenario intubates by Step 4 or 5
- every noncritical scenario ends in the same discharge language
- every critical scenario uses the same generic reassessment-to-ICU rhythm

## Reassessment Guardrail
Reassessment must match the disease mechanism.

Examples:
- obstructive disease: air movement, work of breathing, speech, CO2 trend
- PE/shock: BP, perfusion, mental status, oxygenation
- upper airway: stridor, speech tolerance, airway reserve
- neuromuscular: VC/MIP/cough effectiveness, PaCO2, fatigue
- ARDS: PaO2/FiO2 pattern, compliance, ventilator response
- trauma: breath sounds, hemodynamics, structural findings after intervention

## Author Checklist
Before a rewrite is considered done, confirm:
- the case sounds NBRC-like
- the case still follows the original pathology workflow
- the reassessment targets fit the disease mechanism
- the escalation trigger is pathology-appropriate
- the final disposition is not copied from another family
- the ending feels earned, not templated

## Operational Rule For Future Batches
When rewriting future cases:
- preserve the pathology arc first
- then apply the Case 6 realism layer
- if a case starts sounding like another disease family, stop and rebuild from the pathology outward
