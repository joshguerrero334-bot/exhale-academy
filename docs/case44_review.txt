Exhale Academy CSE Review
Case 44 -- Neonatal Critical (IRDS Surfactant Deficiency Ground Glass)

Intro
Premature infant with surfactant-deficiency respiratory failure requiring CPAP, reassessment, and possible surfactant/intubation escalation.

Stem
Very preterm infant has grunting, retractions, and worsening oxygenation shortly after birth.

Step 1
A 30-week premature infant develops worsening respiratory distress shortly after birth.

The following are noted:
HR 154/min
RR 58/min
SpO2 78%

Which of the following should be evaluated initially? SELECT AS MANY AS INDICATED (MAX 4).

A. Assess work of breathing and overall severity of distress (+2)
B. Review prematurity and surfactant-deficiency context (+2)
C. Auscultate aeration and assess retractions/grunting (+2)
D. Obtain chest radiograph and blood gas data (+2)
E. Delay stabilization until all diagnostics are complete (-3)
F. Assume every neonatal respiratory problem follows the same pathway (-3)

Revealed findings after indicated selections:
- Chest radiograph shows diffuse ground-glass opacity with air bronchograms.
- Prematurity and surfactant-deficiency pattern strongly support IRDS.
- Breath sounds are diminished bilaterally with poor aeration.
- ABG: pH 7.24, PaCO2 58 torr, PaO2 44 torr, HCO3 24 mEq/L.

Step 2
Findings suggest infant respiratory distress syndrome. Which of the following should be recommended FIRST?

A. Begin or continue CPAP with oxygen support and prepare for surfactant/escalation if distress persists (+3)
B. Use oxygen only and defer definitive disease-specific management (-3)
C. Delay escalation planning until prolonged deterioration occurs (-3)
D. Transfer to lower-acuity care after brief early improvement (-3)

Step 3
After initial support, the infant remains on CPAP with persistent distress.

Current findings are:
HR 148/min
RR 54/min
SpO2 86%

Which of the following should be evaluated now? SELECT AS MANY AS INDICATED (MAX 4).

A. Trend gas exchange and overall work of breathing on current support (+2)
B. Reassess whether CPAP remains adequate or failure is developing (+2)
C. Determine whether surfactant or intubation is now required (+2)
D. Review repeat blood gas if oxygen need remains high (+2)
E. Stop close monitoring after one modest improvement (-3)
F. Delay reassessment for several hours (-3)

Revealed findings after indicated selections:
- Repeat gas exchange is needed to judge CPAP failure.
- Chest movement and distress level should be trended closely.
- Surfactant or intubation may be required if support remains inadequate.
- ABG: pH 7.22, PaCO2 60 torr, PaO2 48 torr, HCO3 24 mEq/L.

Step 4
Which of the following should be recommended now?

A. Continue NICU-level respiratory care with surfactant/intubation escalation triggers (+3)
B. Transfer to lower-acuity nursery care now (-3)
C. Discharge after temporary stabilization (-3)
D. Observe without explicit escalation triggers (-2)
