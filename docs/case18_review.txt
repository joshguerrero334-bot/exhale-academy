Exhale Academy CSE Review
Case 18 -- Trauma Critical (Flail Chest + Pulmonary Contusion)

Intro
Blunt chest trauma with severe pain, shallow respirations, and worsening oxygenation requiring high-priority stabilization.

Stem
Trauma patient has severe chest pain, shallow respirations, and progressive hypoxemia after blunt injury.

Step 1
A 29-year-old man is brought to the emergency department after blunt chest trauma.

While receiving O2 by nonrebreathing mask, the following are noted:
HR 132/min
RR 36/min
BP 162/96 mm Hg
SpO2 81%

He is in severe respiratory distress after the injury.

Which of the following should be evaluated initially? SELECT AS MANY AS INDICATED (MAX 3).

A. Inspect chest-wall movement (+2)
B. Auscultate breath sounds (+2)
C. Assess how pain is affecting inspiratory effort (+2)
D. Obtain bedside spirometry (-3)
E. Delay treatment until all imaging is completed (-3)

Revealed findings after indicated selections:
- Paradoxical movement of the left lateral chest is present.
- Breath sounds are decreased on the left.
- Pain is limiting inspiratory effort.

High-score progression: Paradoxical chest movement is present, inspiratory effort is limited by pain, and hypoxemia persists.
Default progression: Assessment is incomplete. Hypoxemia and fatigue worsen.

Step 2
The patient remains severely hypoxemic and is taking shallow breaths because of pain. Which of the following should be recommended FIRST?

A. Administer high-concentration oxygen, provide analgesia, and begin close monitoring (+3)
B. Provide analgesia only and reassess later (-3)
C. Delay oxygen because imaging suggests contusion (-3)
D. Discharge when pain improves (-3)

High-score progression: Pain control improves chest movement slightly, but oxygenation remains impaired.
Default progression: Respiratory distress worsens, and oxygenation declines further.

Step 3
After oxygen therapy and pain control are started, the patient remains tachypneic.

While receiving O2 by nonrebreathing mask, the following are noted:
HR 124/min
RR 32/min
BP 154/90 mm Hg
SpO2 86%

Which of the following should be evaluated now? SELECT AS MANY AS INDICATED (MAX 4).

A. Obtain chest radiograph results (+2)
B. Obtain an ABG (+2)
C. Reassess mental status and work of breathing (+2)
D. Determine whether ventilatory support is now needed (+2)
E. Routine discharge planning (-3)
F. Stop close monitoring after brief improvement (-3)

Revealed findings after indicated selections:
- Chest radiograph reveals multiple left rib fractures with patchy left-sided opacities.
- ABG: pH 7.31, PaCO2 48 torr, PaO2 58 torr, HCO3 24 mEq/L.
- Mental status remains intact, but work of breathing is increasing.
- The patient may require ventilatory support if fatigue worsens.

High-score progression: Chest radiograph and ABG support flail chest with pulmonary contusion, and fatigue persists despite initial therapy.
Default progression: Reassessment is delayed, and respiratory failure worsens.

Step 4
Work of breathing increases, and oxygenation remains poor. Which of the following should be recommended now?

A. Escalate to mechanical ventilation and ICU care (+3)
B. Continue unchanged therapy and reassess tomorrow (-3)
C. Transfer to an unmonitored floor bed (-3)
D. Discharge when oxygenation improves briefly (-3)

High-score progression: Final outcome: flail chest and pulmonary contusion are managed with ventilatory support and ICU care.
Default progression: Final outcome: delayed escalation leads to worsening respiratory failure.
