Exhale Academy CSE Review
Case 19 -- Trauma Critical (Ventilator-Associated Pneumothorax Deterioration)

Intro
Ventilated trauma patient develops abrupt respiratory deterioration requiring bedside ventilator and pleural assessment.

Stem
Intubated trauma patient develops sudden hypoxemia, hypotension, and ventilator alarm changes after trauma.

Step 1
A 52-year-old woman is receiving mechanical ventilation after blunt chest trauma.

Current ventilator settings are:
AC/VC
VT 450 mL
RR 16/min
FiO2 0.60
PEEP 5 cmH2O

The ventilator alarms suddenly activate.

The following are noted:
HR 124/min
BP 86/52 mm Hg
SpO2 79%
EtCO2 60 mm Hg

Which of the following should be evaluated initially? SELECT AS MANY AS INDICATED (MAX 4).

A. Check peak airway pressure and delivered tidal volume (+2)
B. Auscultate breath sounds (+2)
C. Percuss the chest (+2)
D. Pass a suction catheter through the endotracheal tube (+2)
E. Wait for repeat ABGs before assessing the chest (-3)
F. Increase sedation and ignore the alarm pattern (-3)

Revealed findings after indicated selections:
- Peak airway pressure has risen sharply, and delivered tidal volume has fallen.
- Breath sounds are absent on the right.
- Percussion is hyperresonant on the right.
- The suction catheter passes easily through the endotracheal tube.

High-score progression: Peak airway pressure has risen sharply, delivered tidal volume has fallen, breath sounds are absent on the right, and percussion is hyperresonant on the right.
Default progression: Missed ventilator warning signs increase collapse risk.

Step 2
The patient remains severely hypoxemic and hypotensive. Which of the following should be recommended FIRST?

A. Administer 100% oxygen and perform immediate pleural decompression (+3)
B. Obtain chest radiograph before intervention (-3)
C. Increase tidal volume to force oxygenation improvement (-3)
D. Give bronchodilator therapy and reassess later (-2)

High-score progression: Immediate decompression improves oxygenation and hemodynamics.
Default progression: Inadequate intervention worsens respiratory/hemodynamic failure.

Step 3
After immediate pleural decompression, the following are noted:

HR 112/min
BP 98/60 mm Hg
SpO2 88%
EtCO2 48 mm Hg

Which of the following should be evaluated now? SELECT AS MANY AS INDICATED (MAX 4).

A. Reassess breath sounds (+2)
B. Repeat ABG (+2)
C. Trend airway pressure and delivered tidal volume (+2)
D. Obtain CBC with hemoglobin and hematocrit (+1)
E. Stop close monitoring after the first improvement (-3)
F. Delay reassessment for several hours (-3)

Revealed findings after indicated selections:
- Breath sounds are now faintly present on the right.
- ABG: pH 7.28, PaCO2 54 torr, PaO2 64 torr, HCO3 25 mEq/L.
- Peak airway pressure is lower, but the pleural injury still requires definitive management.
- Hemoglobin is 10.2 g/dL and hematocrit is 31%.

High-score progression: Breath sounds are returning on the right, airway pressure is lower, and definitive pleural management is still required.
Default progression: Monitoring gaps leave high risk for recurrent deterioration.

Step 4
The patient remains at risk for recurrent deterioration. Which of the following should be recommended now?

A. Insert a chest tube and continue ICU-level ventilator management (+3)
B. Transfer to a low-acuity floor bed once oxygenation improves briefly (-3)
C. Stop ventilator optimization because decompression was performed (-2)
D. Observe without definitive pleural management (-3)

High-score progression: Final outcome: ventilator-associated pneumothorax deterioration is managed with timely decompression, chest tube placement, and ICU continuity.
Default progression: Final outcome: delayed treatment and poor monitoring cause recurrent instability.
